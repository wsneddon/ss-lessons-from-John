## Eastern Religion Terms

| Term         | Religion                    | Meaning                                                                      |
| ------------ | --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brahman**  | Hinduism                    | Ultimate divine reality underlying all existence.                            |
| **Avidya**   | Hinduism, Buddhism          | Ignorance of ultimate reality; the root error that keeps people in bondage.  |
| **Karma**    | Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism | Actions and their consequences that shape one's future condition.            |
| **Dharma**   | Hinduism, Buddhism          | Duty, righteousness, or living according to the proper order of reality.     |
| **Bhakti**   | Hinduism                    | Loving devotion to God.                                                      |
| **Yoga**     | Hinduism                    | Spiritual disciplines aimed at union with God or liberation.                 |
| **Samsara**  | Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism | The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth.                                      |
| **Moksha**   | Hinduism                    | Liberation from samsara and union with ultimate reality.                     |
| **Dukkha**   | Buddhism                    | Suffering, dissatisfaction, unease, or the instability of earthly existence. |
| **Tanha**    | Buddhism                    | Craving, thirst, attachment, or clinging; the immediate cause of dukkha.     |
| **Nirvana**  | Buddhism                    | The cessation of craving and suffering; freedom from rebirth.                |
| **Haumai**   | Sikhism                     | Ego-centeredness; the illusion of self-rule that separates people from God.  |
| **Waheguru** | Sikhism                     | "Wonderful Lord"; a common Sikh name for God.                                |

### Buddhist Cause-and-Effect

A common Buddhist summary is:

**Avidya (ignorance) → Tanha (craving) → Dukkha (suffering)**

The goal of the Eightfold Path is to remove ignorance and craving, resulting in the cessation of suffering and attainment of Nirvana.
