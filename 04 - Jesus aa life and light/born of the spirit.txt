The concept of being "born of the Spirit" (or closely related ideas like being "born again," "born from above," spiritual regeneration, or new birth by the Holy Spirit) is most prominently introduced in the Gospel of John, particularly in Jesus' conversation with Nicodemus.

In John 3:3-8 (ESV), Jesus says:

    "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God" (v. 3).
    "Unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God" (v. 5).
    "That which is born of the flesh is flesh, and that which is born of the Spirit is spirit" (v. 6).
    "Do not marvel that I said to you, ‘You must be born again.’ ... So it is with everyone who is born of the Spirit" (vv. 7-8).

This emphasizes a spiritual rebirth initiated by the Holy Spirit, distinct from physical birth.

While the exact phrase "born of the Spirit" is unique to John 3 in the New Testament, the same theological concept—spiritual regeneration or new birth through the Spirit—appears in several other passages:

    John 1:12-13 — "But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God."
    This describes believers as born "of God" (a divine, spiritual birth, parallel to being born of the Spirit).
    Titus 3:5 — "...he saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy, by the washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit."
    Here, "regeneration" (Greek: palingenesia, meaning rebirth or new birth) is directly tied to the renewing work of the Holy Spirit, often seen as synonymous with the "born again" experience.
    1 Peter 1:3 — "Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! According to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead."
    This speaks of God causing believers to be "born again" (or begotten anew) through Christ's resurrection.
    1 Peter 1:23 — "...since you have been born again, not of perishable seed but of imperishable, through the living and abiding word of God."
    The new birth is attributed to the imperishable "seed" of God's word, implying a spiritual origin.
    1 John (multiple references) uses "born of God" repeatedly to describe true believers:
        1 John 2:29 — "If you know that he is righteous, you may be sure that everyone who practices righteousness has been born of him."
        1 John 3:9 — "No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him; and he cannot keep on sinning, because he has been born of God."
        1 John 4:7 — "...everyone who loves has been born of God and knows God."
        1 John 5:1 — "Everyone who believes that Jesus is the Christ has been born of God..."
        1 John 5:4 and 5:18 also echo this.

These passages in 1 John emphasize that being "born of God" produces evidence like righteous living, love, and faith in Christ—outcomes of the Spirit's regenerative work.

Other related ideas include:

    2 Corinthians 5:17 — "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come." (A "new creation" implies spiritual rebirth.)
    Galatians 4:29 — Refers to those "born according to the Spirit" (in contrast to the flesh), using Isaac as a type.

Overall, the core teaching is that salvation involves a supernatural, Spirit-initiated new birth, not human effort, enabling entry into God's kingdom and a transformed life. The Gospel of John presents it most explicitly with the "Spirit" terminology, while other books use equivalent concepts like regeneration, new birth, or being born of God.
