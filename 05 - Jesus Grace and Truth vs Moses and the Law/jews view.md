In the first century, gezerot (singular: gezerah) were understood as rabbinic decrees or "fences" around the Torah. These were preventive measures enacted by sages, scribes, or courts (like the Sanhedrin or leading Pharisees) to safeguard against accidental or potential violations of biblical commandments.

Pharisees → Saw gezerot as harmonious extensions of the Torah that protected and fulfilled it.
Jesus → Argued that some traditions elevated human rules above God’s priorities (mercy, compassion, justice, and the true purpose of the Law). He accused them of “teaching as doctrines the commandments of men” (Mark 7:7) and making “void the word of God by your tradition” (Mark 7:13).

Specific Examples You Mentioned

Healing on the Sabbath
Pharisees (and others) viewed healing as “work” prohibited on the Sabbath under their expanded rules (one of the 39 categories of melachot). Jesus repeatedly healed on the Sabbath (e.g., the man with the withered hand in Mark 3:1-6, the crippled woman in Luke 13:10-17).
Jesus countered: “Is it lawful on the Sabbath to do good or to do harm, to save life or to destroy it?” He compared it to rescuing a sheep from a pit — something even strict observers would do. He emphasized that the Sabbath was made for man’s benefit, and “the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath” (Mark 2:27-28). His healings demonstrated God’s compassionate reign breaking into the present, exposing how some gezerot prioritized rigid rules over mercy.
The Lame Man Carrying His Pallet (John 5:1-18)
Jesus healed a man paralyzed for 38 years at the Pool of Bethesda on the Sabbath and told him, “Rise, take up your bed, and walk.” The man obeyed, carrying his mat.
Jewish leaders (likely including Pharisees) said, “It is the Sabbath, and it is not lawful for you to take up your bed” (John 5:10). This violated gezerot about carrying objects between domains or domains on the Sabbath.
The focus of their outrage was not the miraculous healing but the “work” of carrying the mat. Jesus later defended His actions by linking them to the Father’s ongoing work of sustaining life (John 5:17), asserting divine authority. This incident highlights the contrast: the tradition protected the Sabbath’s external form but missed its deeper purpose and the opportunity to celebrate God’s mercy
