Introduction from John 17 (The High Priestly Prayer)
Jesus’ prayer in John 17 is saturated with the theme of glory. It opens with: “Father, the hour has come; glorify your Son that the Son may glorify you” (John 17:1, ESV). Jesus refers to the glory He shared with the Father before the world existed (v. 5), the glory revealed in His earthly work (v. 4), and the glory He gives to believers for their unity and mission (v. 22). Glory here is not abstract but relational, missional, and tied to the cross and resurrection—the “hour” of Jesus’ glorification through suffering and victory.
This lesson explores glory as it relates to God the Father, Jesus, the Holy Spirit (where connected in Scripture), and the chosen/believers.
1. The Words for “Glory”

Hebrew (Old Testament): כָּבוֹד (kabod or kavod) — From the root kabad (“to be heavy”).
English definitions/meanings: Weight, heaviness, importance, honor, splendor, wealth, magnificence. It conveys something of great value or substance that demands respect. In Scripture, it often refers to God’s manifest presence (e.g., filling the tabernacle, Exodus 40:34-35).
Greek (New Testament, including John 17): δόξα (doxa) — From dokeō (“to think, seem, or opine”).
English definitions/meanings: Opinion, reputation, honor, praise, splendor, radiance, brightness, majesty. In the Septuagint (Greek OT) and NT, it expands to the visible manifestation of God’s character, worth, and excellence.

Common uses vs. Scriptural understanding:

Everyday English/Greek classical: “Glory” often means fame, praise, or personal achievement (e.g., “glory in sports” or vainglory).
Biblical: It is God-centered—the intrinsic weight/worth of who God is, displayed in His acts, and worthy of recognition and worship. It is not self-promotion but the public display of divine perfection, often linked to light, presence, and redemptive acts. Humanity falls short of it (Romans 3:23) but is invited to participate through Christ.

2. Glory of God the Father

Key in John 17: Jesus seeks to glorify the Father through His obedience and work (vv. 1, 4). The Father’s glory is the source and goal.
OT Concepts: Kabod fills the tabernacle/temple (Exodus 40; 1 Kings 8); seen in creation (Psalm 19:1), the exodus, and visions (Isaiah 6; Ezekiel 1). God’s glory is jealous for His name (Isaiah 42:8) and will ultimately fill the earth (Numbers 14:21; Habakkuk 2:14).
NT Concepts: The Father is glorified in the Son’s work (John 17; Philippians 2:11). Eternal life is knowing the Father and Son (John 17:3). God’s glory is the radiance of all His perfections (holiness, love, justice, power).
Scriptural Understanding: The Father’s glory is His majestic presence and character, revealed for His people’s good and the world’s knowledge of Him.

3. Jesus’ Glory

Key in John 17: Jesus prays for restoration to pre-incarnate glory (v. 5) and has already glorified the Father on earth. He shares this glory with believers (v. 22). In John’s Gospel, glory is revealed supremely in the cross (John 12:23-24; 13:31-32)—humiliation becomes exaltation.
OT Concepts: Foreshadowed in the kabod of God’s presence and the promised Messiah who reflects God’s glory (e.g., Isaiah 40-55, the Suffering Servant glorified).
NT Concepts: Jesus is “the radiance of the glory of God” (Hebrews 1:3); the Word became flesh and we beheld His glory (John 1:14). Glory is seen in miracles, transfiguration, resurrection, and ascension. Jesus’ glory is both inherent (as eternal Son) and bestowed through the cross.
Scriptural Understanding: Jesus embodies and reveals the Father’s glory. His path to glory goes through the cross, redefining it as sacrificial love rather than worldly power.

4. The Holy Spirit and Glory
The Holy Spirit is not named in John 17, but the prayer is deeply Trinitarian, and Scripture connects the Spirit closely to glory.

Key Connections: The Spirit glorifies Jesus by taking what is His and declaring it (John 16:14). Some interpreters see the “glory” Jesus gives believers (John 17:22) as including or symbolized by the indwelling Spirit—the presence of God in us, enabling unity and witness. The Spirit is a foretaste of future glory.
OT Concepts: The Spirit (or God’s presence/kabod) empowers and fills (e.g., on leaders, in the tabernacle).
NT Concepts: The Spirit reveals and magnifies God’s glory in believers’ lives (2 Corinthians 3:18—transformed from glory to glory); guarantees our inheritance (Ephesians 1:13-14); raised Jesus and will raise us (Romans 8:11).
Scriptural Understanding: The Spirit manifests and applies God’s glory internally, making the invisible visible in the church’s life and unity.

5. Glory of the Chosen (Believers)

Key in John 17: “The glory that you have given me I have given to them, that they may be one even as we are one” (v. 22). This glory fosters unity, love, and witness so the world believes (vv. 21-23). Believers will behold and share in Jesus’ glory (v. 24).
OT Concepts: Israel as God’s people was to reflect His glory (e.g., through obedience and the temple presence), though they often fell short. The kabod was for the community’s benefit.
NT Concepts: Believers are conformed to Christ’s image (Romans 8:29-30), transformed into glory (2 Corinthians 3:18), and will share in eschatological glory (Romans 8:17-18; Colossians 3:4; 1 John 3:2). Glory is both present (through the Spirit) and future (resurrection bodies, seeing God face to face).
Scriptural Understanding: Believers receive derived glory—not inherent like God’s, but participatory through union with Christ. It manifests in holiness, unity, suffering-with-Christ, and mission. The goal is God’s glory displayed in redeemed people.

Common Uses vs. Scriptural Understanding (Summary)

Secular: Glory = personal fame, accolades, or beauty (often fleeting and self-focused).
Biblical: Glory = the heavy weight of God’s perfect character put on display. It humbles us, draws worship, and transforms participants. True glory is found in giving it to God, not seeking it for ourselves (contrast vainglory in Philippians 2:3).
