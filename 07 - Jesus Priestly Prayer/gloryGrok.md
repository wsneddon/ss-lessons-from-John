**Glory in the Bible** refers to the weight, worth, splendor, honor, and radiant majesty of God—often manifested visibly or experienced as His presence and character on display. It contrasts sharply with fleeting human glory and finds its ultimate expression in the mutual glorification between the Father and the Son, with believers invited to share in it through Christ.

### Hebrew and Greek Words for Glory

**Hebrew: כָּבוֹד (kābôd or kabod/kavod)**  
- Derived from the root *kābed* ("to be heavy" or "weighty").  
- Meanings: Weight, heaviness (literal or figurative), worth, honor, splendor, glory, riches, or importance. It conveys something of substance and value that demands respect or awe—often terrifying in its intensity. The opposite is *qālôn* (lightness, shame, dishonor).  
- Key references: Frequently used for God's glory filling the tabernacle (Exodus 40:34-35), appearing as a cloud or fire (Exodus 24:16-17; 16:10), or in calls to give God glory (Psalm 29:1-2; Isaiah 6:3—"the whole earth is full of his glory"). It also applies to human honor or wealth (e.g., Genesis 31:1; Isaiah 61:6).

**Greek: δόξα (doxa)**  
- In classical Greek: Opinion, reputation, or praise. In the Septuagint (Greek OT) and NT, it takes on the Hebrew sense of radiance, splendor, brightness, majesty, and honor—often God's visible or manifest excellence.  
- Meanings: Splendor/radiance (e.g., of stars in 1 Corinthians 15:41), honor/prestige, or the intrinsic worth of God. It ties to *doxazō* (to glorify/honor).  
- Key references: Used ~168 times in the NT. Examples include the glory of the Lord shining around the shepherds (Luke 2:9), Jesus as the radiance of God's glory (Hebrews 1:3), and believers beholding and being transformed by it (2 Corinthians 3:18).

### God's Glory Revealed in the Bible

God's glory is His self-revelation—His holiness, power, beauty, and presence made known. It is not abstract but often tangible:

- **Old Testament examples**: The glory fills the tabernacle (Exodus 40:34-38, preventing entry due to its weight); appears on Mount Sinai like devouring fire (Exodus 24:17); Isaiah sees the Lord high and lifted up, with seraphim crying "Holy, holy, holy" and the earth full of His glory (Isaiah 6:1-3); Ezekiel visions the glory departing and returning (Ezekiel 10; 43). The heavens declare it (Psalm 19:1).
- **New Testament**: An angel's announcement at Jesus' birth (Luke 2:9, 14); the Transfiguration (Luke 9:32); and supremely in Christ (more below).

God's glory is supreme and not to be given to another in the sense of idols or rivals (Isaiah 42:8; 48:11), yet He shares it redemptively through Christ.

### Man's Glory in the Bible

Human "glory" is derivative, temporary, and often contrasted with God's:

- It can mean honor, reputation, beauty, or status (e.g., 1 Corinthians 11:7—man as the image and glory of God; woman as the glory of man in the context of relational reflection; Proverbs 20:29; Isaiah 40:6—"All flesh is grass, and all its glory like the flower of the field").
- Problems arise when people seek glory from one another rather than God (John 5:44; 12:43—"They loved the glory that comes from people more than the glory that comes from God") or when it leads to pride. All have fallen short of God's glory due to sin (Romans 3:23).
- Positive aspects: Believers are called to reflect God's glory through righteous living, but it remains secondary and dependent.

### Jesus, the Father, and Mutual Glorification

The Bible presents a profound interplay: The Father and Son glorify each other, revealing the Trinity's love and unity. Jesus is the perfect revelation of God's glory.

- **John 1:14**: "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth." Jesus manifests the Father's *kabod/doxa*.
- **John 13:31-32**: After Judas leaves, Jesus says, "Now is the Son of Man glorified, and God is glorified in him. If God is glorified in him, God will also glorify him in himself, and glorify him at once." This points to the cross and resurrection as the hour of mutual glorification.
- **John 17 (High Priestly Prayer)**: Jesus prays, "Father, the hour has come; glorify your Son that the Son may glorify you" (v. 1). He recalls the preexistent glory they shared (v. 5) and asks that believers behold it (v. 24). Jesus glorified the Father by completing His work (v. 4); the Father glorifies the Son.

Hebrews 1:3 calls Jesus "the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature." In Christ, God's glory is not distant but incarnate—seen in miracles (John 2:11), suffering, and exaltation.

### Christians Sharing in This Glory

Believers do not merely observe God's glory; through union with Christ, they participate in it:

- **Transformation**: "And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit" (2 Corinthians 3:18). Beholding leads to becoming.
- **Inheritance and future revelation**: Romans 8:17-18, 29-30—we are co-heirs with Christ, sharing His sufferings to share His glory. Present sufferings are not worth comparing to "the glory that is to be revealed in us." God predestines believers to be conformed to Christ's image.
- **John 17:22**: "The glory that you have given me I have given to them, that they may be one even as we are one." Jesus imparts this glory for unity and witness.
- **Eternal perspective**: God calls us "to his own glory and excellence" (2 Peter 1:3); we will stand blameless in His glorious presence (Jude 24) and see Christ as He is (1 John 3:2). The ultimate doxology is "to him be glory in the church and in Christ Jesus throughout all generations, forever and ever" (Ephesians 3:21).

In summary, biblical glory begins with God's heavy, radiant worth (*kabod/doxa*), revealed supremely in Jesus, who glorifies the Father and is glorified by Him. Human glory is real but subordinate and prone to vanity. Through faith in Christ, believers are forgiven for falling short, transformed by beholding Him, and destined to share in the eternal, relational glory of the Triune God—moving from glory to glory for His praise. This is the great hope and motivation of the Christian life.
