### 1. Slavery

**Exodus 21:20-21**  
“Anyone who beats their male or female slave with a rod must be punished if the slave dies as a direct result, but they are not to be punished if the slave recovers after a day or two, **since the slave is their property**.”

**Leviticus 25:44-46**  
“‘Your male and female slaves are to come from the nations around you; from them you may buy slaves. You may also buy some of the temporary residents living among you and members of their clans born in your country, and **they will become your property**. You can bequeath them to your children as inherited property and can make them **slaves for life**, but you must not rule over your fellow Israelites ruthlessly.”

**Atheist argument**: These passages appear to regulate and endorse chattel slavery (permanent, inheritable ownership of people from other nations), including physical punishment short of death. It contrasts sharply with modern views of human rights and equality.

**Context in the Law**: Hebrew debt-slaves had some protections and were generally to be freed after six years (Exodus 21:2, Deuteronomy 15:12-18). Foreign slaves, however, could be permanent property. The Law did place *some* limits compared to surrounding ancient Near Eastern cultures (e.g., no killing slaves outright without consequence).

**Other slavery-related issues**:
- Exodus 21:7-11 — Selling daughters as servants/concubines.
- Exodus 21:26-27 — Slaves freed if their master permanently injures them (eye or tooth).

### 2. Treatment of Women

**Deuteronomy 22:28-29**  
“If a man happens to meet a virgin who is not pledged to be married and **rapes her** and they are discovered, he shall pay her father fifty shekels of silver. He must marry the young woman, **for he has violated her**. He can never divorce her as long as he lives.”

**Atheist argument**: This is often called “forcing the rape victim to marry her rapist.” It seems to treat the woman as property of her father and prioritizes social/financial remedy over the victim’s trauma and consent.

**Deuteronomy 21:10-14** (War brides)  
“When you go to war... and you take captives, if you notice among the captives a beautiful woman and are attracted to her, you may take her as your wife. Bring her into your home... After she has lived in your house and mourned her father and mother for a full month, then you may go to her and be her husband... If you are not pleased with her, let her go wherever she wishes. **You must not sell her or treat her as a slave**, since you have dishonored her.”

**Atheist argument**: This regulates sexual use of female prisoners of war. While it offers some protections (mourning period, no selling into slavery), it still permits forced marriage after conquest.

**Other women-related issues in the Law**:
- **Deuteronomy 22:13-21**: A bride accused of not being a virgin could be stoned if the accusation was proven.
- **Numbers 5:11-31**: The “jealousy ordeal” — a suspected adulterous wife drinks bitter water that would curse her if guilty (physical effects like miscarriage or infertility).
- **Exodus 22:16-17**: Seduction of an unbetrothed virgin requires payment to the father and possible marriage.
- Polygamy was permitted and regulated (e.g., Deuteronomy 21:15-17 on inheritance for sons of multiple wives).
- Women generally had fewer rights in inheritance, divorce, and testimony compared to men.

### 3. Death Penalties

**Leviticus 20:13**  
“‘If a man has sexual relations with a man as one does with a woman, both of them have done what is detestable. **They are to be put to death**; their blood will be on their own heads.”

**Deuteronomy 22:20-21**  
“If... no proof of the young woman’s virginity can be found, she shall be brought to the door of her father’s house and there the men of her town shall **stone her to death**. She has done an outrageous thing in Israel by being promiscuous while still in her father’s house.”

**Exodus 31:14-15**  
“Observe the Sabbath, because it is holy to you. Anyone who desecrates it is to be put to death; those who do any work on that day must be cut off from their people. For six days work is to be done, but the seventh day is a day of sabbath rest, holy to the Lord. **Whoever does any work on the Sabbath day is to be put to death**.”

**Atheist arguments**: These seem extremely harsh by modern standards — capital punishment for consensual sex, premarital sex (by women), and working on Saturday. They portray the God of the Bible as cruel, controlling, and disproportionately punitive.

**Other common death penalty issues in the Law**:
- Adultery (Leviticus 20:10)
- Blasphemy (Leviticus 24:16)
- Witchcraft/sorcery (Exodus 22:18)
- Kidnapping (Exodus 21:16)
- Striking or cursing parents (Exodus 21:15, 17)
- Sacrificing children to Molek (Leviticus 20:2)
- Bestiality (Leviticus 20:15-16)
- False prophecy or leading others to other gods (Deuteronomy 13:1-10)

### Broader Context and Common Atheist Points
Atheists often argue these laws reflect **ancient tribal morality** rather than timeless divine perfection. They point out:
- Many laws treat women, slaves, and outsiders as having lesser value.
- The emphasis on ritual purity, sexual boundaries, and communal holiness was enforced with extreme severity.
- Compared to today’s ethics (individual rights, consent, rehabilitation over retribution), these appear barbaric.

Defenders (Christian and Jewish scholars) usually respond with:
- These were laws for a specific ancient theocratic nation in a brutal era.
- Many laws were **reforms** that improved conditions compared to neighboring cultures (e.g., limits on slavery, protections for war captives).
- The New Testament and later Jewish tradition moved away from literal enforcement of these civil penalties.
