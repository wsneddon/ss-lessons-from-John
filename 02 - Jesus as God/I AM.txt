In the Gospel of John, Jesus makes several famous "I am" statements using the Greek phrase ἐγώ εἰμι (transliterated egō eimi). This is an emphatic form of "I am" because Greek often omits the pronoun egō ("I") when the verb eimi ("am") already implies it—making the inclusion deliberate for emphasis, identification, or theological weight.

ἐγώ (egō) — Strong's Greek G1473 — means "I" or "me" (a primary personal pronoun of the first person, used emphatically when expressed).
εἰμι (eimi) — Strong's Greek G1510 — means "I am," "to be," "to exist," "to happen," or "to be present" (the basic verb expressing being/existence, in the first-person singular present indicative tense).

The full phrase egō eimi can translate as:

Simply "I am" (statement of existence or identity).
"It is I" (in contexts like identification or reassurance).
Or, in theologically loaded cases (especially absolute uses without a predicate), an absolute claim echoing divine self-revelation.

Connection to Old Testament Passages and the Exclusive Sense
The phrase ties strongly to God's self-revelation in the Old Testament, especially Exodus 3:14 (Hebrew: אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה, ehyeh asher ehyeh — "I AM WHO I AM" or "I will be who I will be"), linked to YHWH (from the verb hayah, "to be"), implying eternal, self-existent being.

YHWH itself is widely understood by scholars as the third-person form of the same root — essentially meaning "He is", "He causes to be", or "He will be". The name is a wordplay or deliberate echo:

Exodus 3:14 renders it as ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν (egō eimi ho ōn) — "I am the One Who Is/Existent One." The egō eimi part matches John's usage.

It also echoes Isaiah's exclusive divine claims (e.g., Isaiah 43:10-11, 43:25, 45:18), where God says אֲנִי הוּא (ani hu, "I am He"), rendered in LXX as ἐγώ εἰμι (egō eimi). These declare: "I and only I am God"—no other gods, sole savior, eternal existence.
In John, the emphatic egō eimi (especially absolute forms like John 8:58) deliberately echoes this, claiming Jesus shares that exclusive divine identity: eternal, self-existent, unique. The "I and only I" force comes from context and OT parallels, not the words alone (which can be ordinary identification elsewhere). Reactions (e.g., stoning in John 8:59) show the audience understood it as a claim to be God. This is central to John's theology: Jesus embodies the one true God's self-revealed name and being ("I AM" from Exodus via Isaiah), exclusively providing what only God can. No one else holds this status.

John uses egō eimi more frequently than other Gospels (around 24 times total), with two main categories:
Predicated "I Am" Statements
These include a metaphor or title after egō eimi, revealing Jesus' role or identity. The emphatic structure often carries an exclusive sense: "I, and only I, am..." (implying sole/ultimate fulfillment—no other provides this).

John 6:35 (also 6:41, 48, 51):
	"I am the bread of life."
	(Exclusive: I alone am the true source of spiritual sustenance.)
John 8:12:u
	"I am the light of the world."
	(Exclusive: I alone dispel darkness/sin.)
John 10:7, 9:
	"I am the door/gate of the sheep."
	(Exclusive: I alone provide legitimate access/salvation.)
John 10:11, 14:
	"I am the good shepherd."
	(Exclusive: I alone shepherd perfectly, laying down my life.)
John 11:25:
	"I am the resurrection and the life."
	(Exclusive: I alone conquer death and give eternal life.)
John 14:6:
	"I am the way, the 	truth, and the life."
	(Exclusive: I alone am the path to the Father.)
John 15:1 (also 15:5):
	"I am the true vine."
	(Exclusive: I alone am the genuine source of life/fruitfulness.)

These seven are the classic "predicated I Am" sayings. The emphatic egō eimi underscores Jesus as the sole or ultimate embodiment—no rivals or alternatives.
