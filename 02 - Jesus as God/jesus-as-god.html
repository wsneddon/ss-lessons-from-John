A. Testing the Spirits — 1 John 4:1–3
1. The Passage
a. 1 John 4:1–3 — "Test the spirits… every spirit that confesses that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is from God"
b. John responds to early Gnostic/Docetic teaching: Jesus was only divine spirit; physical incarnation was impossible for God
c. John's boundary: true teaching confesses that Jesus Christ truly came in the flesh
Further Study: What "Come in the Flesh" Means
1. Full Humanity of Jesus
a. Jesus had a real human body; truly entered history; His suffering and death were real
2. Real Identity of Christ
a. "Jesus Christ" = the Messiah, the Anointed One — not merely a teacher but the promised Savior
3. Connection to Deity
a. John 1:1 — "the Word was God"; John 1:14 — "the Word became flesh"
b. Colossians 2:9 — "in Him the fullness of deity dwells bodily"
c. Philippians 2:6–7 — Christ pre-existed and became human
d. Logic: if Jesus is truly the eternal Word → He is divine; if He truly became flesh → the incarnation is real; therefore: fully God and fully man
Further Study: What Counts as Heresy Here?
1. Historic Orthodox Christianity (Catholic, Orthodox, most Protestant)
a. Core belief: Jesus is fully God, fully man, truly incarnate
b. Denying the true incarnation places someone outside historic orthodoxy — Christology is essential to the gospel
2. Major Categories of Rejected Teaching
a. Denying Jesus is truly God (e.g., Arianism)
b. Denying Jesus is truly human (Docetism / Gnosticism)
c. Splitting Christ into two separate beings
d. Rewriting the apostolic gospel — changing the meaning of incarnation or salvation
3. Why This Matters
a. If Jesus is not truly God → He cannot fully reveal God
b. If Jesus is not truly human → He cannot redeem humanity
c. Christology is not secondary doctrine — it is foundational to salvation

Further Study: Is Genesis 3:15 a Reference to the Virgin Birth?
1. The Passage — Protoevangelium ("First Gospel")
a. Genesis 3:15 — "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel." (ESV)
2. Modern Biblical Scholarship
a. "Seed of the woman" is generally read as a collective reference to humanity or the woman's descendants
b. Hebrew zeraʿ ("seed") typically means offspring/descendants — not specifically tied to reproductive biology
c. Most scholars see the passage as describing ongoing hostility between humans and evil (symbolized by the serpent), not predicting a virgin birth
d. The virgin birth connection is considered a later theological interpretation, not the original authorial intent
3. Traditional Christian Theology
a. Early church fathers (Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, Augustine) interpreted Genesis 3:15 as the first Messianic prophecy
b. The unusual phrase "seed of the woman" — genealogy normally traces through the father — was taken as suggestive of the virgin birth
c. Fulfilled in Christ: connected to Isaiah 7:14 ("the virgin shall conceive"), Matthew 1:18–25, and Luke 1:26–35
d. Viewed retrospectively as part of a larger pattern of prophecy pointing toward Christ's victory over evil
4. A Helpful Distinction
a. Original (historical) meaning: the struggle between humanity and evil — not originally about the virgin birth
b. Later theological (canonical) reading: a proto-Messianic promise, sometimes connected symbolically to the virgin birth

Further Study: The Greek egō eimi and Old Testament Background
1. The Greek Phrase
a. ἐγώ εἰμι (egō eimi) — an emphatic "I am"; Greek normally omits the pronoun since the verb implies it, making its inclusion deliberate
b. ἐγώ (egō) — Strong's G1473 — "I" or "me"; a primary personal pronoun, used emphatically when expressed
c. εἰμι (eimi) — Strong's G1510 — "I am," "to be," "to exist"; first-person singular present indicative of the verb "to be"
d. The phrase can mean: "I am" (identity/existence), "It is I" (reassurance), or in absolute uses — a claim echoing divine self-revelation
2. Connection to the Old Testament
a. Exodus 3:14 — God reveals his name as אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה (ehyeh asher ehyeh, "I AM WHO I AM"), linked to YHWH — eternal, self-existent being
b. YHWH derives from the verb hayah ("to be") — essentially "He is" or "He causes to be" — a deliberate echo of the same root
c. Septuagint (LXX) renders Exodus 3:14 as ἐγώ εἰμι ὁ ὤν (egō eimi ho ōn) — "I am the One Who Is/Existent One" — directly matching John's usage
d. Isaiah's exclusive divine claims use אֲנִי הוּא (ani hu, "I am He"), rendered in LXX as ἐγώ εἰμι:
— Isaiah 43:10–11 — "I and only I am God… no savior besides me"
— Isaiah 43:25 — "I am He who blots out your transgressions"
— Isaiah 45:18 — "I am the LORD, and there is no other"
3. John's Absolute Uses
a. John 8:58 — "Before Abraham was, I am" — no predicate; pure claim to eternal existence
b. The audience's reaction (picking up stones, John 8:59) confirms they understood it as a claim to be God
c. The "I and only I" force comes from OT context, not the words alone — Jesus embodies YHWH's self-revealed name
4. Echoes in Revelation
a. Revelation 1:4 — "from him who is and who was and who is to come"
b. Revelation 1:8 — "says the Lord God, who is and who was and who is to come, the Almighty"
c. Revelation 4:8 — "Holy, holy, holy, is the Lord God Almighty, who was and is and is to come"
