Church Fathers' Views (2nd–4th centuries AD)
Early Christian writers uniformly attributed the Gospel of John to John the Apostle (son of Zebedee, also called the “disciple whom Jesus loved” or the Beloved Disciple). They described it as the last of the four Gospels, written in Ephesus in Asia Minor late in John’s life, after the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, and Luke) had circulated. It was often called a “spiritual Gospel” emphasizing deeper theological truths alongside historical events.
Key examples include:

Irenaeus (c. 180 AD, Bishop of Lyons, disciple of Polycarp who knew the apostles): “John, the disciple of the Lord, who also had leaned upon His breast, did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus in Asia.” He presented this as reliable tradition from those who knew the apostles directly.
Clement of Alexandria (c. 215 AD): John, having seen the other Gospels focus on external facts, was urged by friends and inspired by the Spirit to write a “spiritual Gospel” after his oral ministry in Asia.
Origen (c. 185–254 AD) and Eusebius (c. 260–340 AD): Both affirmed apostolic authorship by John the Apostle (distinguishing him from any other “John” where relevant). Eusebius quoted earlier sources and listed the Gospel as undisputed in the church canon.
Other early attestations came from Tertullian, the Muratorian Canon, and Justin Martyr. A small group (the Alogi) rejected it for theological reasons tied to Montanism/Gnosticism, but this was not the mainstream view.

Later writers like Jerome echoed this tradition. The Church Fathers saw the Gospel as based on eyewitness testimony from the Apostle, composed in Ephesus toward the end of the first century.
Modern Scholarly Views on Authorship
The Gospel itself is formally anonymous—it never names its author but refers to the “disciple whom Jesus loved” (the Beloved Disciple) as the source of its testimony (see John 19:35; 21:24: “This is the disciple who is testifying to these things and has written them, and we know that his testimony is true”). Some verses shift to first-person plural (“we have seen his glory,” John 1:14), suggesting the author or final editor claims connection to eyewitness events.

Majority scholarly consensus (critical/biblical studies mainstream): The author is not John the Apostle (the Galilean fisherman). Reasons include the Gospel’s sophisticated Koine Greek style and theology (far beyond what an “unlettered” Aramaic-speaking fisherman from Galilee, per Acts 4:13, would likely produce), its strong focus on Judean (not Galilean) details and rabbinic interpretation, and its distinct presentation of Jesus compared to the Synoptics (e.g., longer discourses, “I am” statements, different chronology). Scholars generally view it as the product of a Johannine community or “school”—followers of the Beloved Disciple who edited and expanded his eyewitness traditions, possibly over time (with possible first and second editions). The Beloved Disciple was a real historical figure (not purely symbolic), but his exact identity is unknown; candidates include the Apostle John, Lazarus, a Jerusalem priest, or another unnamed disciple. The final form likely comes from a close companion or editor of this disciple.
Minority/traditionalist views (often more conservative or evangelical scholars): Some defend (or see strong evidence for) direct or close connection to John the Apostle, citing the early and consistent patristic testimony, internal eyewitness-like details (e.g., realistic Jerusalem topography and chronology), and arguments that the Greek style could come from a secretary/amanuensis or long reflection. Figures like Leon Morris, John A.T. Robinson, and others have supported this or a very close link. Richard Bauckham and others emphasize the Beloved Disciple as an eyewitness source.

Debates about a possible “John the Elder” (mentioned by Papias, c. 130 AD) as author or source have largely faded; many now see Papias as referring to the Apostle John himself.
Overall, while the Gospel claims a strong eyewitness foundation through the Beloved Disciple, most scholars see it as a theological reflection shaped by a later community rather than a direct apostolic memoir.
Modern Scholarly Views on Date
The broad consensus is c. 90–100 AD (sometimes 90–110 AD), making it the latest of the four canonical Gospels. It was likely composed (or finalized) in Ephesus. Supporting factors: developed Christology (suggesting time after the Synoptics), references implying post-70 AD context (e.g., John 11:48), and the earliest manuscript fragment (P52) dating to around 125–150 AD, which sets an upper limit. Gnostic writers like Heracleon commented on it by the mid-2nd century.
A minority of scholars (including some conservatives like John A.T. Robinson, and more recently voices like N.T. Wright, Richard Bauckham, and others) argue for an earlier date (pre-70 AD, possibly 60s–70s). They point to intimate pre-destruction knowledge of Jerusalem (e.g., pools and topography that some once thought were invented), the lack of any explicit reference to the Temple’s fall in 70 AD (which the author might have highlighted if writing afterward), and possible independence from the Synoptics. A few even propose stages of composition beginning earlier. However, this remains a minority position; the 90s AD date is still dominant.
In short, the Church Fathers present a clear, unanimous tradition of apostolic authorship in Ephesus late in the first century. Modern scholarship largely accepts the late-1st-century date and Ephesian origin but sees the text as emerging from a community tied to an eyewitness (the Beloved Disciple) rather than directly from the Apostle John’s pen. Debates continue, especially around the exact role of eyewitness tradition versus later theological development.
