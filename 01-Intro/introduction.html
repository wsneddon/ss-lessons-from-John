B. The Book
1. Authorship and Date
a. Church Fathers' Views (2nd–4th centuries AD) — unanimous attribution to John the Apostle
— Written in Ephesus late in John's life, after the Synoptic Gospels had circulated
— Often called a "spiritual Gospel" emphasizing deeper theological truths
— Irenaeus (c. 180 AD, disciple of Polycarp who knew the apostles): "John, the disciple of the Lord… did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus in Asia"
— Clement of Alexandria (c. 215 AD): urged by friends and inspired by the Spirit to write a "spiritual Gospel" after his oral ministry in Asia
— Origen (c. 185–254 AD) and Eusebius (c. 260–340 AD): both affirmed apostolic authorship; listed as undisputed in the church canon
— Also attested by Tertullian, the Muratorian Canon, and Justin Martyr
— Small dissenting group (the Alogi) rejected it for theological reasons — not the mainstream view
b. Modern Scholarly Views on Authorship
— The Gospel is formally anonymous — never names its author
— Refers to the "disciple whom Jesus loved" as source of testimony (John 21:20–24; 13:23)
— Majority consensus: not directly authored by John the Apostle; product of a Johannine community or "school"
— Reasons: sophisticated Greek style, strong Judean focus, distinct theology vs. Synoptics
— The Beloved Disciple was a real historical figure; exact identity unknown (candidates: John, Lazarus, a Jerusalem priest)
— Minority/traditionalist view: Leon Morris, John A.T. Robinson, Richard Bauckham support direct or close connection to John the Apostle
c. Modern Scholarly Views on Date
— Broad consensus: c. 90–100 AD — latest of the four canonical Gospels, likely composed in Ephesus
— Earliest manuscript (P52) dated c. 125–150 AD sets the upper limit
— Gnostic writers like Heracleon commented on it by the mid-2nd century
— Minority: some conservatives (Robinson, Wright, Bauckham) argue pre-70 AD based on detailed Jerusalem topography and no explicit reference to Temple's fall
—Very late 2nd-century dates largely faded after manuscript evidence (e.g., P52 ~125–150 CE) showed the text already existed early in the 2nd century. Today’s critical consensus (including Ehrman) favors late 1st century (~90–100 CE), not mid-2nd.
d. Passage missing from early manuscripts
— John 7:53–8:11 (the story of the woman caught in adultery, or Pericope Adulterae) Not present in any Greek manuscripts before the 5th century; style and vocabulary differ from the rest of John. Modern translations usually bracket it or footnote: “The earliest manuscripts do not include 7:53–8:11.”

C. About the Author
1. Life of John
a. Born c. AD 6–15, Galilee (likely Bethsaida or Capernaum); son of Zebedee and Salome; brother of James the apostle
b. Called among the first disciples; inner circle with Peter and James
c. The "Beloved Disciple" — remained at the crucifixion; entrusted with Mary's care
d. Pillar of the Jerusalem church (Galatians 2:9); founded churches in Asia Minor
e. Exiled to Patmos under Emperor Domitian (c. AD 95) — wrote Revelation there
f. Released under Nerva (AD 96–98); returned to Ephesus
g. Died naturally in Ephesus c. AD 100 — only apostle not martyred
2. Christian Tradition
a. Attributed authorship: Gospel of John, 1–3 John, Revelation
b. Known for teaching: "Little children, love one another" (Jerome)
c. Plunged into boiling oil in Rome under Domitian — emerged unharmed; then exiled to Patmos
— Earliest written reference: Tertullian (c. 200 AD), who mentions it as a well-known miracle
d. Fled Ephesian bathhouse on seeing the heretic Cerinthus
e. Disciple chain: John → Polycarp → Irenaeus — key line of apostolic tradition
3. John in the Gospels and Acts
a. Early Ministry
— Called by the Sea of Galilee with brother James (Matt 4:21–22; Mark 1:19–20; Luke 5:10)
— Named "Sons of Thunder" (Boanerges) by Jesus (Mark 3:17)
— Inner circle witness to raising of Jairus' daughter (Mark 5:37; Luke 8:51)
— Sent out with the Twelve (Matt 10; Mark 6:7; Luke 9:1)
— Asked to call down fire on a Samaritan village; Jesus rebukes him (Luke 9:51–56)
b. Mid-Ministry
— Witnesses the Transfiguration with Peter and James (Matt 17:1–9; Mark 9:2–10; Luke 9:28–36)
— Reports stopping an unauthorized exorcist; Jesus corrects him (Luke 9:49–50)
— Requests seats of honor in Christ's kingdom (Matt 20:20–28; Mark 10:35–45)
c. Passion Week
— Sent with Peter to prepare the Passover meal (Luke 22:8)
— The "Beloved Disciple" reclining next to Jesus at the Last Supper (John 13:23–25)
— Among the three in Gethsemane during Jesus' agony (Matt 26:36–37; Mark 14:32–33)
— Gains access to the high priest's courtyard during Jesus' trial (John 18:15–16)
— Present at the cross; Jesus entrusts Mary to his care (John 19:25–27)
d. Resurrection Events
— Runs with Peter to the empty tomb and believes (John 20:2–8)
— First to recognize the risen Jesus by the Sea of Galilee (John 21:7)
e. In the Book of Acts
— Listed among apostles waiting in the Upper Room (Acts 1:13)
— Accompanies Peter in healing the lame man at the Temple (Acts 3:1–11)
— Stands boldly with Peter before the Sanhedrin (Acts 4:1–22)
— Lays hands on new Samaritan believers with Peter (Acts 8:14–17)
f. Summary
— Consistently part of the inner circle (Peter, James, John)
— Present at key revelation moments: Transfiguration and Resurrection
— Witness to the full Passion narrative
— Early leader in the Jerusalem church
